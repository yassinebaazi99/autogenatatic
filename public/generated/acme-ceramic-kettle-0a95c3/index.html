Common Questions

Before you brew

Everything worth knowing about the Acme Ceramic Kettle — materials, care, and how it fits into your morning ritual.

Can I use this on an induction stovetop? +

The Acme Ceramic Kettle is stovetop-safe on gas and electric coil or glass-top ranges. It is not compatible with induction cooktops, since the ceramic body doesn't contain a ferromagnetic layer. If induction is all you have, you'd need to heat water separately and transfer it.

How does the walnut handle hold up over time? +

The handle is turned from solid American walnut and finished with a food-safe oil. It stays cool during stovetop use and develops a deeper patina with age. We recommend re-oiling it with mineral oil every few months — the same way you'd care for a wooden cutting board.

1.2 liters — is that enough for multiple cups? +

Comfortably. A standard pour-over uses roughly 300–350 ml of water. At full capacity, the kettle gives you enough for three solid brews before refilling — or one generous batch on a Chemex 6-cup. Most mornings, you'll fill it once.

Will ceramic crack or chip if I set it down too hard? +

The body is high-fire stoneware — denser and more impact-resistant than standard pottery. It handles daily use well. That said, it's ceramic, not steel. Dropping it onto tile from counter height could cause damage. Treat it the way you'd treat a favorite mug and it'll last years.

How does this compare to a Fellow or Hario gooseneck? +

Those are excellent stainless-steel kettles with built-in thermometers and electric bases. The Acme takes a different approach: ceramic retains heat longer and pours with a naturally slower flow rate, which many pour-over enthusiasts prefer for an even bloom. At $89, it's also meaningfully less expensive than most electric goosenecks. The trade-off is no temperature readout — pair it with a simple probe thermometer if precision matters to you.

Does it whistle? I live in a small apartment. +

No whistle. The open gooseneck spout means water heats quietly — no startling your roommate or your cat at 6 a.m. You'll want to keep an eye on it or set a timer, but your neighbors will thank you.
